Phase 1: Foundations

The Birth of a Profession

Industrial Design is the strategic intersection of Art, Engineering, and Business. Its birth was predicated on a radical shift in energy and economy.

James Watt & The Steam Engine

The Steam Engine provided the massive, consistent energy required to power global factories and railroads. It created the concentration of labor and capital necessary for industry to demand a role that could bridge the machine and the human: The Designer.

"We have machines that can make anything, but we don't know what they SHOULD look like."

Philosophy: 10 Principles of Good Design

Dieter Rams

Concerned by an "impenetrable confusion of forms," Dieter Rams established these principles at Braun to ensure design serves the user with absolute honesty. They are the "10 Commandments" of modern industrial design.

  • 01.Good design is innovative

    Technical development always offers new opportunities for design. Innovative design always develops in tandem with innovative technology, and can never be an end in itself.

  • 02.Good design makes a product useful

    A product is bought to be used. It has to satisfy certain criteria, not only functional, but also psychological and aesthetic. Good design emphasizes the usefulness whilst disregarding anything that could possibly detract from it.

  • 03.Good design is aesthetic

    The aesthetic quality of a product is integral to its usefulness because products we use every day affect our person and our well-being. But only well-executed objects can be beautiful.

  • 04.Good design makes a product understandable

    It clarifies the product’s structure. At best, it is self-explanatory. It makes the product talk. A great design doesn't need a manual.

  • 05.Good design is unobtrusive

    Products fulfilling a purpose are like tools. They are neither decorative objects nor works of art. Their design should therefore be both neutral and restrained, to leave room for the user’s self-expression.

  • 06.Good design is honest

    It does not make a product more innovative, powerful or valuable than it really is. It does not attempt to manipulate the consumer with promises that cannot be kept.

  • 07.Good design is long-lasting

    It avoids being fashionable and therefore never appears antiquated. Unlike fashionable design, it lasts many years – even in today’s throwaway society.

  • 08.Good design is thorough, down to the last detail

    Nothing must be arbitrary or left to chance. Care and accuracy in the design process show respect towards the user.

  • 09.Good design is environmentally friendly

    Design makes an important contribution to the preservation of the environment. It conserves resources and minimizes physical and visual pollution throughout the lifecycle of the product.

  • 10.Good design is as little design as possible

    Less, but better – because it concentrates on the essential aspects, and the products are not burdened with non-essentials. Back to purity, back to simplicity.

Phase: 1953 - 1968 | Germany

HfG Ulm (Ulm School)

The Ulm Model

The **Hochschule für Gestaltung Ulm** (Ulm School of Design) was the spiritual successor to the Bauhaus. It moved away from "Design as Art" and toward a strictly **scientific, objective, and sociological approach**.

Designers were seen as "co-ordinators" of technology, sociology, and economics. This period birthed the rigorous grid systems and minimalist interfaces we see in modern computing today.

Key Contributions

Ulm collaborated heavily with **Braun**, defining the look of German post-war technology. They created the "Ulm Stool" and the standardized visual languages for companies like Lufthansa.

"Design is not a self-expressive art; it is a serious professional discipline based on science and logic."
×
Landscape

Scientific Objectivity

The Driver

Post-war West Germany needed high-quality, standardized products to rebuild its economy and identity. The school focused on human factors, ergonomics, and semiotics.

Impact

It institutionalized Industrial Design as a professional academic subject, separate from the Fine Arts traditions of the earlier Bauhaus.

Phase: 1981 - 1987 | Milan

Memphis Group

The Postmodern Rebellion

Named after a Bob Dylan song, the Memphis Group was a collective of designers in Milan who hated the "boring" functionalism of the 70s. They believed that **form does NOT follow function—it follows emotion**.

They used inexpensive "kitsch" materials like plastic laminate and neon colors to create objects that were more like sculptures than tools.

Key Contributions

Led by **Ettore Sottsass**, mereka created the "Carlton" shelving unit and numerous "Radical" designs that questioned why objects have to be "good" or "serious" at all.

"A product is like a character in a play. It should talk, perform, and have its own personality."
×
Landscape

The PM Rebellion

The Driver

By the 1980s, the "International Style" of glass and steel had become corporate and sterile. Memphis was a sensory explosion against this austerity.

Impact

It validated "bad taste" as a valid design choice and paved the way for the semantic, playful design of the 1990s and the early digital era.
